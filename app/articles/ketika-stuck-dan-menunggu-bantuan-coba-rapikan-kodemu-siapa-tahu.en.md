We've all been there — it's late at night, there's a mysterious bug, your brain has hit a wall, and Stack Overflow has nothing for you. ChatGPT can't help either because you don't even know what to ask.

In moments like that, there's one simple trick that often gets overlooked: **clean up your code.** Not just to make it look pretty, but to genuinely restructure it — give variables clearer names, break long functions into smaller ones, remove dead code.

Why?

## 1. It Makes the Problem More Visible

Bugs love to hide inside spaghetti code. Once you clean up the logic flow, suddenly: _Oh, this condition never actually gets reached!_

## 2. You Re-Understand What You Actually Wrote

Cleaning forces you to read through it line by line. It's like making your brain rewind from the beginning — and chances are, you'll spot the mistake yourself before any help arrives.

## 3. You Help Your Helper Help You

If you do end up asking someone for help, clean code is like a clear map. They can jump straight to the core of the problem without having to decode what was going on inside your head.

## 4. Practice Thinking Clearly Under Pressure

Refactoring while stressed isn't fun. But that's exactly what makes you more resilient. This is the mindset of a developer who can hold it together: even in chaos, you stay systematic.

## 5. You Might Just Fix It Yourself…

And this happens more often than you'd think: after tidying things up here and there, the bug disappears. Turns out it wasn't some deep logic error — just a small typo. But you could only see it _after_ your code was actually readable.

---

So next time you're stuck and have no idea what to do next, don't immediately throw your laptop across the room or fire off a "hey can you help?" message. Try refactoring first. Maybe you don't need help at all — you just need a more organized version of yourself.

**You never know, right?**
